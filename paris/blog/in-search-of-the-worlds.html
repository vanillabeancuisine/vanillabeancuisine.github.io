In Search of the World’s Best Beer

Previous
Next
List

One of the grand and wonderful things about living in Paris is that the travel options from here are just so incredible, especially to Americans like us where it takes a pretty big effort to get to another country. Hop on a train, take a short flight, or head out on a drive, and here in Europe you can be speaking a different language and experiencing an entirely different culture within hours! So we are both excited and paralyzed by the options around us: Italy? the U.K.? Spain? Turkey? Germany? There are literally too many places and not enough time. That said, we knew coming here that Belgium would be one of the first places on our travel itinerary. Why, you ask? Well, let it be known that my husband has grown from his humble Wisconsin roots into a full-blown beer snob. In the course of his “research", he has become a huge fan of both American-style IPAs (which are très difficile to find here) and Belgian beer. And while you *can* ferret out good beer here in Paris, the whole craft beer movement currently sweeping the States has not yet arrived. To the extent that Chuck has mostly switched to drinking wine here and for his birthday last month, I had to venture out to a few specialty shops in order to find some good beer that he would really enjoy. (Is it wrong that when the kids put together a treasure hunt for his birthday, the final clue led him to a great big bag of beer?) 

Anyway, at some point he came across a story about the “best beer in the world” (according to RateBeer.com) made by an obscure abbey in Belgium. The monks at the Abbey of St. Sixtus of Westvleteren make beer to support their abbey, and have the lowest production of the 10 Trappist monasteries that make beer (and who, in turn, produce way less beer than say, Budweiser.) Besides that, the beer is notoriously hard to find. The abbey is 2 hours from Brussels and not on any major transportation line. You can only buy the beer in the café/gift shop across the street from the abbey or through making a reservation to pick up a case of beer at what-I-shall-call-the “abbey-drive-thru” (it’s like McDonald’s, except for awesome beer!). And, the phone line to make the reservation is always booked up since they apparently receive as many as 85,000 calls an hour. Did you say impossible quest to find the world’s best beer? Challenge accepted! Chuck made it his mission to find a way to get that beer, and one Monday morning, was able to reserve his case. (How, you ask? Well I could tell you, but I’d have to kill you. Or actually, Chuck could tell you. I have no frickin’ idea except that it took up the better part of a weekend.)

After that, it was my turn to kick it into high gear by renting a car and reserving an AirBnB apartment in Bruges. (As driving was really the only option for the monastery, and we had heard lovely things about Bruges, we decided to spend the weekend there before picking up our beer at Westvleteren on our way home on Monday.) We picked up our rental car and headed out Friday afternoon after picking up the girls from school. The drive was not pleasant—traffic leaving Paris was awful, the USB iPhone charger in the car kept going in and out, the car’s helpful navigation system (albeit in French) disappeared as soon as we crossed the border into Belgium, and I somehow confused the directions on my iPhone and ended up taking us a good half hour out of our way before we realized we were not, in fact, 5 minutes from Bruges. Damn you, technology! I would have killed for a good old triptik 2.5+ hours into our trip… But ultimately we made it and were soon eating and enjoying our first of many good beers. 

Besides the chilly, windy, rainy weather (ah, Europe, I’m getting sick of this refrain in these postings), Bruges was wonderful. We stayed in a recently renovated four-level narrow townhouse, just a few minutes walk from the town square. Kitchen on the ground floor, living room with gas fireplace on the first floor, master bedroom and bath on the 2nd floor, then a little attic room under the eaves for the girls. 

We ate our fill of Belgian waffles, took a boat ride down the canals of Bruges, admired the beautiful architecture on foot, and explored the many delicious chocolate shops. (See more pictures in my album here.) 

Saturday morning, we walked to the town farmers' market where I bought ingredients to make breakfast the next day. The girls stood for a long while petting the baby chicks and rabbits, while I bought fresh eggs from the farmer. 

We all enjoyed an interactive museum called “The Sound Factory” where we could play bells like those that ring in the nearby churches and take turns at music stations featuring Macs, an electronic dome, and tall metal poles. And, of course, we ate and drank well. We had a delicious traditional Belgian meal (mussels and fries for Chuck, and a yummy beer-infused stew—with fries!—for me) at this cute little restaurant off one of the main streets, goofy children notwithstanding.  

But before long, it was time to get our last taste of street waffles and make our pilgrimage to the beer abbey. 

This time the car USB-charger refused to work entirely, and there were several road closings on very small little roads that we had to navigate around without really knowing where the detours would take us. At one point we could see what we thought was the right road about 1/4 mile away, and we may or may not have driven across a one-lane tractor path through some fields to get to it. Ultimately we got to the town, but then couldn’t find the abbey anywhere. Between the iPhone and some very small signage on a couple of roads, we finally made it to the abbey a mere 5 minutes before our beer appointment. Phew! 15 minutes in the drive-thru line (the brew-thru! anyone remember those from the Outer Banks in North Carolina? in retrospect, it seems ill-advised), and 2 cases of the best beer in the world were ours. We celebrated with a late lunch at the café across the street, where we got to try the two other beers the abbey makes, and bought a case of those as well. In the end, we had a trunk full of beer and had to cram the rest of our stuff in various parts of the rental car. Worth it. And now accepting bribes should we manage to get some home to the United States. Cheers!
